1. EPISTEMOLOGICAL POSITIONING: SYNTAX AS UNIVERSAL MODELING LANGUAGE

Research Proposition: This case study demonstrates that the hierarchical principles of syntactic theory, specifically X‑Bar Theory, provide a universal modeling language for organizing complex information systems. By applying syntactic hierarchy to corporate process design, the research reveals that syntax is not merely a linguistic faculty but a cognitive architecture for structuring any complex, multi‑level system. The work bridges theoretical linguistics and operational optimization through systematic structural mapping.

2. RESEARCH CONTEXT: LINEAR TEXT VS. HIERARCHICAL PROCESSING

The Empirical Problem

  • Data Source: Real corporate call‑center procedures in a multinational telecommunications company, documented as linear text files
  • Scale: Thousands of daily customer‑agent interactions requiring rapid access to hierarchical decision protocols
  • Problem: Linear text formats forced cognitive "scrolling" and violated natural decision‑making hierarchies
  • Consequence: Increased handling time, operational errors, and agent frustration due to structural misalignment between documentation and cognitive processing

Theoretical Framework

  • Syntactic Theory as Modeling Language: X‑Bar Theory (XP → X′ → X) provided a universal template for hierarchical organization
  • Structural Isomorphism: Identified equivalence between syntactic constituents and process components (CP → IP → VP)
  • Cognitive Alignment: Designed process flows that mirror natural human decision‑making (binary branching, locality, projection)

Cognitive Violence

Linear texts required constant scrolling and context‑loss during phone interactions

Flat Structure

Questions, answers, and actions intermixed without visual hierarchy

Procedural Scrambling

Illogical step ordering (e.g., "check email" before "does the cliente have internet access?") violating canonical resolution sequences

Internal Research Finding: "Agents took too long to locate information during customer calls and hated 'scrolling' through screens. Processes with 24+ steps generated excessive handling times and operational errors."

Syntactic Problems Identified:

  • Flat Structure: Text is a linear list without logical branching, forcing mental "parsing."
  • Complements and Adjuncts Mixed: Essential actions ("check speakers") appear alongside contextual notes ("customers with TV").
  • Procedural Scrambling: "Update drivers" (complex action) precedes "check volume" (basic diagnosis), violating canonical resolution order.
  • EPP (Extended Projection Principle) Violation: Process begins with actions without clearly establishing context ("What's the problem?").

Can the customer hear the video audio?
Procedure for adjustment (IP[YES]):

1. Check volume configuration; guide the customer to adjust it to maximum
2. Test the speaker/headphone connection
3. Suggest testing with other videos/music
4. If needed, walk them through a driver update

If the issues gets fixed, thank them for contacting and end the call. If not, return and select NO.
Procedure for ticket (IP[NO]):

1. Inform the customer that you will open a technical case
2. Register: Complaint → Broadband → Services → Y‑Video
3. Say they will receive a response within 5 business days
4. Thank them for contacting and finish the call

Syntactic Principles Applied:

  • X‑Bar Hierarchy Respected: CP (question) → IP (YES/NO bifurcation) → VP (actions).
  • Syntactic Selection (C‑selection): The question (C°) selects exactly two complements (IP[YES] and IP[NO]).
  • [±YES] Features Valued: The agent's choice values uninterpretable features, allowing the derivation to converge.
  • Locality (Minimal Link Condition): Each decision leads directly to relevant actions, without cognitive "jumps."
  • Theta Criterion Satisfied: Each scenario (argument) has a defined action sequence (thematic role).
