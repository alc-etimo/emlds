1. INTRODUCTION: TRANSFORMING LANGUAGE INTO INFRASTRUCTURE

Core Proposition: This case study documents the design and implementation of a linguistic data governance pipeline in a real corporate environment. The project emerged from the need to mediate between spoken customer language, AI-assisted systems, and institutional knowledge bases in large-scale customer support operations.

At its core, this work addresses a fundamental problem in Linguistic Data Science: how to transform highly variable, context-dependent human language into structured, explainable, and ethically governed data without erasing linguistic diversity. By integrating speech analytics, ontological modeling, and corporate taxonomy design, this project positions language not merely as input for AI systems, but as infrastructural data requiring explicit governance.

Real Corporate Environment

Production-level implementation in multinational telecommunications customer support

Linguistic Mediation

Bridging spoken customer language, AI systems, and institutional knowledge bases

Infrastructure Perspective

Treating language as governable infrastructure rather than mere surface communication

2. CONTEXT AND PROBLEM DEFINITION

The Operational Challenge

  • AI-Mediated Support Ecosystem: Customer support operations where AI tools assisted 30,000+ agents during live calls
  • Dual Data Streams: Speech analytics captured customer speech while tracking agent search terms in CRM systems
  • Persistent Linguistic Bottleneck: Despite advanced AI, language variability caused systematic failures in information retrieval
  • Transparency Deficit: Automated systems lacked explainability regarding linguistic interpretation

The Linguistic Data Problem

  • Variability as Structural Issue: Regional expressions, synonyms, and informal patterns disrupted AI performance
  • Cognitive Load: Agents struggled with linguistic mediation between customers and rigid systems
  • Search Failures: Natural language diversity resulted in delayed resolutions and poor user experience
  • Governance Gap: Language was processed but not governed in AI pipelines
Key Insight: Language variability, rather than technical performance, became the primary obstacle to effective AI-mediated decision-making in customer support. This revealed a fundamental gap in how linguistic data was structured and governed within automated systems.

3. RESEARCH QUESTIONS AND HYPOTHESES

Guiding Research Questions

  • How can spoken language data be transformed into structured knowledge without suppressing linguistic variation? → This question addresses the tension between standardization needed for computational systems and preservation of natural language diversity.
  • Can terminology governance improve information retrieval in AI-assisted environments? → Investigates whether explicit linguistic governance mechanisms can enhance rather than hinder AI performance.
  • What role do ontologies play in mediating between human language and automated systems? → Examines ontological modeling as a bridge between unstructured speech and structured computational processing.
Central Hypothesis: Linguistic governance — implemented through controlled vocabularies and taxonomies — could reduce noise in AI pipelines while increasing transparency, ethical accountability, and operational efficiency. This hypothesis positioned governance not as constraint but as enabling infrastructure.

7. FROM GLOSSARY TO LINGUISTIC INFRASTRUCTURE

Conceptual Evolution: The project originated as a practical glossary for content standardization but evolved into governed infrastructure as the scope of linguistic inconsistency became evident. This transition marked a paradigm shift: language ceased to be treated as editorial surface and became recognized as infrastructural data.

Initial Phase: Glossary

  • Purpose: Standardize writing practices among content creators
  • Scope: ~700 terms with basic definitions
  • Perspective: Language as editorial concern
  • Governance: Style guide recommendations

Evolved State: Linguistic Infrastructure

  • Purpose: Govern AI-system interactions and information retrieval
  • Scope: 2,700+ terms with conceptual relations and validation
  • Perspective: Language as structural component of systems
  • Governance: Formal mechanisms with ethical accountability
Infrastructural Turn: Linguistic decisions transformed from stylistic preferences to governance mechanisms with direct impact on AI behavior, user experience, and ethical accountability. This shift reflects the core premise of Linguistic Data Science: language is data that requires explicit modeling and governance when operationalized in computational systems.
